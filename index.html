7 Spanish Mistakes You're Probably Making Right Now

After more than a decade teaching Spanish to students from over 30 nationalities, I've heard the same mistakes come up again and again. The good news? Once you know what to look for, these errors are surprisingly easy to fix.

1. Gender agreement — the silent killer

Every Spanish noun is either masculine or feminine, and adjectives must match. The problem is that endings can be misleading. Words like "el problema" (masculine) and "la mano" (feminine) break the pattern beginners expect.

COMMON ERRORS VS. CORRECT FORMS
✗ "una problema grande" → ✓ "un problema grande"
✗ "la agua fría" → ✓ "el agua fría" (feminine noun, masculine article before stress)
✗ "la foto bonita" → ✓ this one IS correct — "foto" is feminine
TEACHER'S TIP

Pro tip: Always learn new nouns with their article — never just "problema", always "el problema". This wires the gender into your memory from day one.

2. Ser vs. Estar — the eternal battle

Both mean "to be", but they're not interchangeable. Ser is for permanent or defining characteristics; Estar is for states, conditions, and locations.

SIDE BY SIDE
"Soy médico." (I'm a doctor — identity)
"Estoy cansado." (I'm tired — temporary state)
"Es aburrida." (She's a boring person — personality)
"Está aburrida." (She's bored right now — current state)

3. Por vs. Para — both mean "for", until they don't

  • Por = purpose, destination, deadline: "Este regalo es para ti", "Salgo para Madrid", "Lo necesito para el lunes"
  • Para = cause, duration, exchange, agent: "Gracias por tu ayuda", "Trabajé por tres horas", "Fue escrito por Cervantes"

4. The false cognate trap

Spanish and English share thousands of similar-looking words — but some of them are traps. These "false friends" have caught out every single one of my students at some point.

FALSE COGNATES THAT WILL EMBARRASS YOU
"Embarazada" ≠ embarrassed — it means PREGNANT
"Molestar" ≠ to molest — it means to bother/annoy
"Sensible" ≠ sensible — it means sensitive
"Actual" ≠ actual — it means current/present

5. Pretérito Indefinido vs. Imperfecto

English learners tend to use the simple past (comí, fui) for everything. But Spanish has two past tenses that do very different jobs: Indefinido for completed actions, Imperfecto for habits, ongoing states, and descriptions.

SPOT THE DIFFERENCE
"Ayer comí pizza." (I ate pizza yesterday — one completed action)
"De niño comía pizza cada viernes." (As a child I used to eat pizza every Friday — habit)
"Cuando llegué, llovía." (When I arrived, it was raining — ongoing background)
TEACHER'S TIP

Quick test: if you can say "used to" or "was doing" in English, you probably need Imperfecto. If it's a one-off completed event, use Indefinido.

The Subjunctive Mood: A Clear, No-Nonsense Guide

The subjunctive is the aspect of Spanish grammar that tends to separate intermediate learners from advanced ones. It's not taught well in most courses, and that creates unnecessary fear. Let's fix that.

What IS the subjunctive?

The subjunctive is a mood (not a tense). Spanish has three moods: Indicative (objective facts), Subjunctive (doubt, wishes, emotions, hypotheticals), and Imperative (commands). The key insight: the subjunctive doesn't describe reality — it describes your attitude toward something.

INDICATIVE VS. SUBJUNCTIVE IN ACTION
"Creo que tiene razón." (I think he's right – I believe it's true – Indicative)
"No creo que tenga razón." (I don't think he's right – I doubt it – Subjunctive)
"Sé que viene mañana." (I know she's coming – fact – Indicative)
"Espero que venga mañana." (I hope she comes – desire – Subjunctive)

The WEIRDO framework

Language teachers have used the WEIRDO acronym for decades because it works. If the main clause falls into one of these categories, the subordinate clause goes into subjunctive:

  • W — Wishes: querer que, desear que, esperar que...
  • E — Emotions: alegrarse de que, temer que, sorprender que...
  • I — Impersonal expressions: "Es importante que...", "Es necesario que..."
  • R — Recommendations: recomendar que, aconsejar que, sugerir que...
  • D — Doubt / Denial: dudar que, no creer que, negar que...
  • O — Ojalá (and other wish expressions): "¡Ojalá venga!", "¡Quizás llegue!"
TEACHER'S TIP

One crucial rule: subjunctive only kicks in when there are TWO DIFFERENT SUBJECTS. "Quiero ir" (I want to go – same subject = infinitive). "Quiero que vayas" (I want you to go – different subjects = subjunctive).

How to conjugate the present subjunctive

The rule is elegant: take the YO form of the present indicative, drop the -o, and add the "opposite" endings (-e for -AR verbs, -a for -ER/-IR verbs).

CONJUGATION PATTERN
hablar → hablo → hable, hables, hable, hablemos, habléis, hablen
comer → como → coma, comas, coma, comamos, comáis, coman
venir → vengo → venga, vengas, venga, vengamos, vengáis, vengan

The key irregulars (worth memorising)

  • ser → sea, seas, sea, seamos, seáis, sean
  • ir → vaya, vayas, vaya, vayamos, vayáis, vayan
  • haber → haya (used in compound tenses)
  • saber → sepa, sepas, sepa...
  • estar → esté, estés, esté...
TEACHER'S TIP

Practice exercise: "Espero que mi profesor ____ (ser) paciente." / "Es importante que ____ (estudiar, tú) todos los días." / "No creo que ella ____ (saber) la respuesta." Answers: sea / estudies / sepa.

Spain's Linguistic Map: Why the Country Speaks More Than One Language

When people sign up to "learn Spanish", they generally mean Castilian — the Spanish spoken across most of the country and across Latin America. But Spain is, in fact, a plurilingual state, and knowing its linguistic map is as important as knowing its geography.

Castilian (Castellano)

The official language of the entire country. Born in the Kingdom of Castile during the Middle Ages. It spread through the Iberian Peninsula and, after 1492, across Latin America. Today it's spoken by over 500 million people as a first language worldwide — making it the second most spoken native language on Earth.

Catalan

Around 9 million speakers. Co-official in Catalonia, the Balearic Islands, and the Valencian Community (where it's called Valencian). It's also the sole official language of Andorra, and is spoken in parts of southern France and the Sardinian city of Alghero. Catalan is more similar to French and Italian than to Castilian.

QUICK CATALAN VS. CASTILIAN COMPARISON
Good morning: "Bon dia" (Castilian) / "Bon dia" (Catalan)
Thank you: "Gracias" / "Gràcies"
I don't understand: "No entiendo" / "No ho entenc"

Galician (Gallego)

Spoken by around 2.4 million people in Galicia (northwest Spain). It's closely related to Portuguese — both descend from Medieval Galician-Portuguese. If you speak Portuguese, you'll understand a great deal of Galician with no formal study.

Basque (Euskera)

The most fascinating language in Western Europe. Basque is a language isolate — it has no proven relationship to any other language in the world. Not Latin-derived, not Indo-European, not related to any of its neighbors. Its origin remains one of linguistics' great mysteries. Spoken by approximately 750,000 people in the Basque Country and parts of Navarre.

TEACHER'S TIP

Why does this matter for learners? If you're planning to live or work in Barcelona, Valencia, Bilbao, or Santiago, showing basic awareness of the local language is enormously valued — even if you only know a few words. It signals respect, and doors open.

Dialects within Castilian Spanish

  • Andalusian: aspirated or dropped -s, seseo/ceceo in the south
  • Canarian: resembles Caribbean Spanish more than mainland Spanish
  • Murcian: preserves archaic vocabulary and Arabic-influenced terms
  • Standard ELE (the Spanish I teach): based on the educated norm of central-northern Spain

30 Essential Spanish Phrases for Travellers (That Actually Get Used)

Most travel phrasebooks teach you "where is the train station?" and "how much does this cost?". Useful, but locals can tell immediately when someone is reading from a script. Here are phrases that actually sound natural — the ones I'd tell my friend before their first trip to a Spanish-speaking country.

Polite openers that change everything

START CONVERSATIONS LIKE A LOCAL
"Disculpe, ¿me puede ayudar?" — Excuse me, could you help me? (much warmer than just "perdona")
"¿Habla inglés por casualidad?" — Do you happen to speak English? (polite, not demanding)
"Estoy aprendiendo español, así que perdone los errores." — I'm learning Spanish, so forgive my mistakes. (locals LOVE this)
"¿Cómo se dice ... en español?" — How do you say ... in Spanish?

At restaurants (the most important category)

  • "¿Qué me recomienda?" — What do you recommend? (simple, works everywhere)
  • "Soy alérgico/a al gluten / a los frutos secos / al marisco." — I'm allergic to gluten / nuts / shellfish.
  • "¿Está incluido el servicio?" — Is service/tip included?
  • "¿Me puede traer la cuenta, por favor?" — Could you bring me the bill, please?
  • "¡Está buenísimo!" — It's delicious! (more enthusiastic than "está rico")

Getting around & emergencies

  • "¿Me puede indicar cómo llegar a ... ?" — Can you tell me how to get to ... ?
  • "Tengo prisa / estoy perdido / me duele mucho." — I'm in a hurry / I'm lost / it hurts a lot.
  • "¿Dónde está la farmacia / el hospital más cercano?" — Where is the nearest pharmacy / hospital?
TEACHER'S TIP

Always smile and add "por favor" and "gracias". A friendly "Disculpe" opens more doors in Spain and Latin America than any phrasebook sentence.

Spanish Cinema: 8 Films That Will Transform Your Listening Comprehension

Passive exposure to Spanish through film is good. Active, strategic watching is transformative. The difference is in how you approach it — not just sitting back and watching, but engaging with the language as a learner. Let me show you the method I give all my students from B1 upwards.

The 3-pass method

  • Pass 1: Watch with Spanish subtitles. Don't pause. Let the story carry you and absorb the rhythm.
  • Pass 2: Watch again without subtitles (or with Spanish CC only). Focus on what you catch and what you miss.
  • Pass 3: Watch with pauses. Write down 5-10 new expressions or structures you heard. Look them up, then re-watch the scene.

Curated starter list (A2–B2)

RECOMMENDED FILMS
• "El laberinto del fauno" (fantasy + historical, clear diction)
• "Volver" (Almodóvar – everyday Spanish, emotional)
• "Ocho apellidos vascos" (comedy, modern slang + regional)
• "Relatos salvajes" (Argentine, fast & colloquial)
• "Mar adentro" (clear, moving, great for listening to slower speech)
TEACHER'S TIP

Choose films whose topic genuinely interests you. Motivation beats "educational" value every time. One film watched three times with the method above is worth ten watched passively.

How to Build Spanish Vocabulary That Actually Sticks

The most common complaint I hear from students is: "I learn new words and then forget them within days." This is not a memory problem — it's a method problem. Memory follows predictable rules, and once you understand them, vocabulary retention becomes a skill you can actually control.

Spaced repetition: the single most powerful technique

Your brain consolidates information when you revisit it at increasing intervals. Cramming 50 words the night before a class creates an illusion of learning — but the words are gone within 72 hours. Spaced repetition forces your brain to retrieve a word just as you're about to forget it, each time making the memory stronger.

Context beats lists every time

Memorising "otoño = autumn" gives you one memory hook. Learning "En otoño, las hojas de los árboles se vuelven rojizas y amarillas" gives you grammar, context, collocations, and emotion — five hooks instead of one.

CONTEXT-RICH VS. ISOLATED LEARNING
Isolated: "tener prisa" = to be in a hurry
In context: "¡Date prisa, que llegamos tarde!" (¡No tenemos tiempo. — Hurry up, we're late!)
The second version teaches you tener prisa, darse prisa, llegar tarde, etc. all at once.

Exploit Spanish's word families

Spanish has rich morphology. If you learn one root, you can derive many words from it. This multiplies your vocabulary without extra effort.

WORD FAMILY: TRABAJAR (TO WORK)
trabajo (noun: work/job)
trabajador/a (adj / noun: hardworking / worker)
trabajoso/a (adj: laborious, demanding)
trabajar (verb: to work)

Your instant vocabulary shortcut: cognates

If your native language is English, you already know roughly 20,000 Spanish words. English borrowed heavily from Latin and French — and so did Spanish. Words ending in -tion in English usually end in -ción in Spanish: information → información, communication → comunicación, etc.

TEACHER'S TIP

Daily practice that works: write 3–5 sentences in Spanish at the end of each day about what you actually did. Use words you're trying to learn. This forces active production — the deepest form of learning. Even at A1: "Hoy he comido pasta. Estaba buena. Mañana tengo clase de español."
